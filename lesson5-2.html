Networking Devices


Networking devices are hardware components essential for establishing, managing, and securing communication within a network. They facilitate the exchange of data between devices like computers, servers, and mobile devices.

(NIC) Network Interface Card

- A Network Interface Card, commonly referred to as a NIC, is a device that allows computers to be joined together in a LAN, or Local Area Network.
    * Networked computers communicate with each other using a given protocol or agreed-upon language for transmitting data packets between the different machines, known as nodes.
- The Network Interface Card acts as the liaison for the machine to both send and receive data in a LAN.                    
                
Ethernet NIC RJ 45
- Network Interface Card with an RJ45 port, used for connecting to Ethernet networks via twisted-pair cables.
                        

Ethernet NIC BNC-Coax
- Network Interface Card with a BNC (Bayonet Neill-Concelman) connector, used for older Ethernet networks with coaxial cables.
                        

Wireless NIC
- Network Interface Card that connects devices to wireless (Wi-Fi) networks without cables.
                        

Fiber Optic NIC
- Network Interface Card designed to connect to fiber optic networks, providing high-speed data transfer through light signals in
fiber optic cables


HUB

- It is the basic connector for a network, as a junction for the computers to connect together.
- If all the cards are of the same speed, 10Mbps vs. 10/100Mbps cards, buying a simple hub to match their speed is ideal and most economical.
- It is a fairly unsophisticated broadcast device
 * Hubs do not manage any of the traffic that comes through them.
    * A hub is not an intelligent device, unlike an ethernet switch.


Ethernet Switch

- Switches intelligently route data between network segments, offering greater efficiency than hubs. They enable devices with mismatched speeds to
communicate effectively and are crucial for large networks and data centers.

Role of Switches in a Network

- Switches serve as network connection points for hosts at the network's edge.
- In hierarchical network architectures, switches are also used deeper in the network to connect edge switches.
- Commercial switches may have built-in or modular interfaces to connect diverse network types like Ethernet, Fibre Channel, RapidIO, ATM, ITU-T G.hn, and 802.11.
- Connectivity is possible across different network layers:
    - Layer 2: Handles bandwidth-shifting within the same technology.
    - Layer 3: Facilitates interconnecting diverse technologies (e.g., Ethernet and token ring) via routing, typically performed by routers.
- Modern switches may support Power over Ethernet (PoE) to supply power to devices like VoIP phones and wireless access points without separate power sources.
- Redundant power circuits in switches, connected to uninterruptible power supplies (UPS), ensure continued device operation during power failures.

Form Factors of Switches

Switches come in various form factors for different environments:
> Stand-alone desktop units: Used in home or office settings.
> Rack-mounted switches: Installed in equipment racks or enclosures, available as standalone, stackable, or large chassis units with swappable line cards.
> DIN rail-mounted switches: Designed for industrial environments.
> Small installation switches: Mounted in cable ducts, floor boxes, or communication towers, often used in fiber-to-the-office setups.

Configuration Options

Unmanaged Switches:
- No configuration interface or options.
- Plug and play.
- Least expensive, typically used in small office/home office environments.
- Available in desktop or rack-mounted formats.

Managed Switches:
- Offer configurable management options.
- Can be managed via CLI, SNMP, or a web interface.
- Allow configuration of features like Spanning Tree Protocol, port mirroring, port bandwidth, and VLANs.

Smart Switches:
- A type of managed switch with a limited set of management features.
- Typically cheaper than enterprise switches.

Enterprise Managed Switches:
- Full management features (CLI, SNMP, web interface).
- Allow advanced configurations, such as backup, restore, and detailed optimizations.
- More expensive and commonly used in larger networks where centralized management is important.
- Stackable versions are available for more extensive networks.

Web-Managed Switches:
- A middle ground between unmanaged and managed switches.
- Provide basic configuration via a web interface, but no CLI access.
- Less expensive than fully managed switches.


Router

- Routers direct data packets between different networks, acting as gateways. They analyze data packets and route them efficiently while providing security by controlling data flow.


Works of the Routers

- Essential for routing traffic between networks, facilitating the movement of data packets.
- Acts as a gateway between networks, contributing to both data direction and network security.

Main purposes:
1. Directing Data: Ensures data reaches the correct destination (e.g., email to the right provider and recipient).
2. Security: Prevents unauthorized data distribution, protecting network performance (e.g., stopping large files from being sent to all machines on a network).

Wireless Router

- Wireless Router (Wi-Fi Router): Combines the functions of a router and a wireless access point, providing access to the Internet or a private network.
- Usage: Commonly used in homes, connecting users to the internet via cable or xDSL from ISPs.
- Functionality:
    - Replaces wires with wireless radio signals.
    - Can function as a router, switch, and access point.
    - Supports both wired and wireless networks, or a mixed setup.
- Design: Typically small boxes with antennas to improve signal range.
- Limitations: Signal strength weakens with distance from the router.
- Capacity: Supports varying numbers of users, from a few to hundreds, depending on the model.

What does Wi-Fi mean?

- A wireless networking technology using radio waves to provide high-speed Internet access.
- Wi-Fi is a brand name created by marketing firm Interbrand for the founding member of the Wireless Ethernet Compatibility Alliance (now called the Wi-Fi Alliance).

- Wi-Fi Hotspot: A location where Wi-Fi-enabled devices can connect to the Internet.
- Wi-Fi Standards: Defined by the IEEE 802.11 standard, with amendments over time that support various frequencies, bandwidths, and channels.
- Wireless Access Point (WAP or AP): Allows wireless devices to connect to the network.
- Function: Similar to an amplifier, it extends the Wi-Fi range, enabling devices to connect from farther distances.
- Additional Roles: Provides data about devices on the network, enhances security, and serves other practical purposes.

Wireless Router Types


Desktop Wi-Fi Router
- Common way to connect to the Internet wirelessly.
- Typically small boxes with antennas to broadcast the signal.
- Signal strength weakens with distance, often requiring multiple routers for extended coverage.
                        

Mobile Hotspot
- Feature on smartphones that allows sharing of the wireless network connection with other devices, enabling Internet access.
                        

Portalbe Wi-Fi
- A type of mobile hotspot, a small device that broadcasts a 3G or 4G signal for multiple devices to use.
- Offered by cellular carriers, with monthly costs based on the selected data plan.
- Provides a more reliable internet connection compared to public Wi-Fi hotspots.


Cabling

- Nearly all hubs and network cards sold today are what are often called 10base-T or 100base-T.
    - That simply means you use cables called UTP Cat5 24 AWG, they have connector ends on them that resembles a telephone connector.
    - The telephone connector is actually smaller, called an RJ-11 connector, while a 10base-T connector is an RJ-45.
    - But, they are nearly alike, except for size.
- UTP cable can only support the distance up to 100 meters, more than that a repeater or a hub will be useful.

UTP CAT5 24 AWG

- It stands for the Unshielded Twisted Pair Category 5 with 24 American Wire Gauge
- This is the type of cable which is commonly used in networking.
- Although there are many cables out in the market, this is the most suitable for building a network.

RJ-45 Connectors

- RJ-45 is short for Registered Jack45, an eight-wire connector used commonly to connect computers onto Local Area Networks (LANs), especially Ethernets.
- RJ-45 connectors look similar to the ubiquitous RJ-11 connectors used for connecting telephone equipment, but they are somewhat wider.

Cable Types

1. Straight-Through Cable (ST)

- It is a type of twisted-pair copper wire cable for Local Area Network (LAN) use for which the RJ-45 connectors at each end have the same pinout (i.e., the arrangement of conductors).
- It is commonly referred to as patch cable.
- It is used to connect computers and other end-user devices (e.g., printers) to networking devices such as hubs and switches.
- In short, this type of cable is used to connect DIFFERENT devices.                    
                

        > T-568A Standard
        - TIA/EIA-568-A-1995 (Commercial Building Telecommunications Wiring Standards)
        - It defines a standard for building cable system for commercial buildings that support data networks, voice, and video.
        - It also defines the technical and performance criteria for cabling.                    
                



        > T-568B Standard
        - TIA 568-B.1-2000 (Commercial Building Telecommunications Wiring Standard)
        - The year 2000 update packages all the previous addendums and service updates into a new release and, most important, specifies that Category 5E cable is the preferred
        cable type that can provide minimum acceptable performance levels.
        - Several addendums were also released that specify technical information for 100-ohm twisted-pair cable, shielded twisted-pair cable, and optical fiber cable.

2. Crossover Cable

- It is used also in LANs, basically, and is often used in home networks when connecting two Ethernet computers without a hub.
- It has its send and receives wires crossed.
- It is a type of cable that is used to connect SAME or SIMILAR devices such as PC to PC, Hub to Hub, or Switch to Switch.

Cable Interference

1. (EMI) Electro-Magnetic Interference

- EMI is interference caused by electrical or magnetic radiation from nearby sources such as power cables, heavy machinery, or fluorescent lighting.
- Effects: Causes disruptions in signal transmission or reception, leading to Radio Frequency Interference (RFI).
- Prevention:
    - Keep network cables at least 12 inches away from electrical cables.
    - Use shielded cables instead of Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP) if necessary.
- Impact: While it may not significantly affect signal quality, it can be problematic if field strength exceeds 1 Volt per meter.

2. Noise

- Noise is unwanted electrical interference that distorts or disrupts the signal. It can be either temporary (transient) or constant.
- Impact: Affects the clarity of the desired signal, much like static or interference on a radio.

3. Crosstalk

- Crosstalk is interference where signals from one cable induce EMI in an adjacent cable.
- Causes: Result from capacitive, inductive, or conductive coupling between circuits or channels.
- Prevention:
    - Twisting the wires in twisted-pair cables helps reduce crosstalk.
    - Additional shielding or physical separation between cables can further minimize interference.
- Impact: Significant in structured cabling, audio electronics, and other communication systems.

How to Prevent Noise in Cables?

1. Shielding

- Shielded cables are used to protect the data being transferred through the cable from degradation by EMI exposure, which is common in data
centers, industrial settings, offices, and other settings where computer technology, electrical equipment, or electronic equipment is in heavy use.

2. Cancellation

- The twisting of wires causes EMI and crosstalk signals to cancel itself out.
- The main difference between the different categories of UTP is the quality of the copper that is used to make the conductors and how tightly twisted the wires are together.

Toolset Used For Terminating Cables


Crimping Tool
- A crimping tool is a tool designed to crimp or connect a connector to the end of a cable.
- Examples:
    Network cables and phone cables are created using a crimping tool to connect the RJ-45 and RJ-11 connectors
    to the end of the cable.
                        

Cutter Pliers
- These are pliers that are intended for the cutting of wire (they are generally not used to grab or turn anything).
                        

Wire Stripper
- It is a pair of opposing blades much like scissors or wire cutters.
- The center notch or hollow makes it easier to cut the insulation without cutting the wire.
- This type of wire stripper is used by rotating it around the insulation while applying pressure in order to
make a cut around the insulation.
